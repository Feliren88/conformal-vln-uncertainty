\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Vision-and-Language Navigation (VLN) asks an agent to reach a goal described in
natural language, turning a sentence like ``go past the kitchen and stop at the
second door on your right'' into a sequence of moves through a real
building~\cite{anderson2018r2r}. Transformer agents have pushed Room-to-Room
(R2R) success rates into the $60$ to $75\%$
range~\cite{chen2022duet,chen2021hamt,hong2021vlnbert,zhang2024survey}, and the
task now spans high-level instructions, continuous control, and several
languages~\cite{ku2020rxr,krantz2020vlnce,zhu2021soon}. The headline number
hides the failure that matters for a robot sharing space with people. When the
agent misreads an instruction it does not hesitate. It commits to a confidently
wrong turn with no internal sign that anything went wrong, and the wrong turn
compounds step by step into a wrong room.

A deployed navigation agent therefore needs to know when it is unsure, so a
person in the loop can be queried before the mistake
compounds~\cite{abraham2025ask,ren2023knowno}. The obvious signal, the policy's own softmax, is not trustworthy for this,
since modern networks are systematically
overconfident~\cite{guo2017calibration}, and richer estimators, from
dropout and ensembles to selective
classification~\cite{gal2016dropout,lakshminarayanan2017ensembles,geifman2017selective}
and the probabilistic state of classical
robotics~\cite{thrun2005probabilistic}, certify nothing in finite samples
about a set the operator can act on at a risk level the operator sets.

Conformal prediction (CP) supplies exactly that certificate, a prediction set
around any score that contains the correct action with probability at least
$1-\alpha$, distribution-free and valid in finite
samples~\cite{vovk2005alrw,angelopoulos2023gentle}. That guarantee has carried
CP into robot decision-making, from help-seeking
manipulation~\cite{ren2023knowno} and LLM planner
alignment~\cite{liang2024introspective} to model predictive control and
multi-agent motion planning~\cite{lindemann2023conformal,dixit2023multiagent}.

The standard scores run into two problems on navigation. First, the guarantee
empties out. A well-trained policy is sharply peaked, the mean top-action
probability on R2R val-unseen runs from $0.71$ to $0.84$ across the backbones
we test, so the standard classification
scores~\cite{sadinle2019least,romano2020aps,angelopoulos2021raps} pile up near
zero, the calibrated threshold stops responding to $\alpha$, and on the most
concentrated policies it drops to all-singleton sets that carry no uncertainty
at all (Section~\ref{ssec:failure}). Second, VLN is a walk, so the steps of
one episode are dependent and are not exchangeable, which breaks the
assumption plain split CP is built on, and the move from seen buildings to
unseen ones is a covariate shift that pulls coverage away from
target~\cite{tibshirani2019covariateshift,barber2023beyond}. The first effect
we remove by construction, making the walk itself the calibration unit; the
second we measure rather than assume away.

Our answer is a parameter-free proxy for step difficulty, the policy's own
residual confidence $1-\pmax$. Dividing any base score by $1+(1-\pmax)$
rescales it by one positive scalar per step, widening the set where the policy
is unsure and leaving confident steps alone, without changing the order of
candidates and without a single trained parameter. Paired with a calibration
unit defined over the whole episode rather than the step, it restores the
threshold's response to $\alpha$ at the cost of one subtraction per step, and
the guarantee it rests on is proved in full immediately after the method.

Coverage alone is not deployment. If covering a hard step forces the set to
span the aisle and the robot to ask for confirmation every few seconds, the
warehouse stops moving; safety and throughput pull against each other, and we
make that trade-off explicit, including a closed loop in which an operator
answers only the steps the sets flag.

\paragraph*{Contributions}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{A parameter-free conformal score with an episode-level unit}
(Section~\ref{sec:method}). We divide any base score by $1+(1-\pmax)$ and
calibrate over an episode-level maximum. It holds no trainable parameters, adds
no latency, and restores the response to $\alpha$ that the base scores lose to
overconfidence (Section~\ref{ssec:failure}).
\item \textbf{A complete proof that coverage holds at every level}
(Section~\ref{sec:guarantee}). Theorem~\ref{thm:cov} gives exact finite-sample
simultaneous coverage over the whole walk for every $\alpha$ the operator
selects, with ties allowed and the steps of an episode free to depend on each
other arbitrarily; exchangeable episodes are the single premise.
\item \textbf{Validation across five backbones and two tasks}
(Section~\ref{sec:exp}). On the agents of DUET~\cite{chen2022duet},
HAMT~\cite{chen2021hamt}, and Recurrent VLN-BERT~\cite{hong2021vlnbert} with
three base scores, the normalised score holds step coverage above target in all
$45$ runs, including the concentrated backbones where the base scores collapse,
its threshold transfers across a $16.4$-point success-rate spread to within
$0.019$, a measured total variation of $0.156$ records the seen-to-unseen
price, and the method carries to REVERIE~\cite{qi2020reverie} on the DUET and
HAMT agents.
\end{enumerate}
