\begin{abstract}
A robot that follows a spoken route instruction can be confidently wrong,
and conformal prediction (CP) promises a distribution-free guarantee that
the correct move sits inside a calibrated prediction set at an
operator-chosen risk level. On navigation the standard scores break that
promise. A well-trained policy is sharply overconfident, so the scores pile
up near zero, the calibrated threshold stops moving with the risk level and
collapses to uninformative singletons, and pooling the dependent steps of
one walk voids the guarantee. We restore it with a parameter-free
normalisation that divides any base score by one plus the policy's own
residual confidence, calibrated over whole episodes, and we prove in full
that the correct move stays inside the set at every step of the walk with
probability at least $1-\alpha$, in finite samples, at every level
$\alpha$, with exchangeable episodes as the single premise. Across five
backbones reproduced to within one point of their published success rates,
the score holds step-averaged coverage above target in all $45$ runs, its
threshold transfers across a $16.4$-point success-rate spread, and the
guarantee carries to REVERIE navigation while the grounding head loses
coverage only to the seen-to-unseen shift, recovered by recalibration.
Closing the loop, an operator answering the steps the sets flag lifts
success from $71$ to $91$ percent at a $32$ percent ask rate. The reading
for deployment is to act on the step-level coverage, recalibrate inside
each new building, and reach for the cheap score.
\end{abstract}
