\begin{abstract}
Vision-and-language navigation (VLN) agents that misread an instruction
commit to a confidently wrong turn with no internal sign of trouble.
Conformal prediction (CP) promises the missing safeguard, a
distribution-free, finite-sample guarantee that the correct action lies in a
calibrated prediction set at an operator-chosen risk level, but we show that
the standard CP scores break this promise on navigation: well-trained
policies are sharply overconfident, so the scores concentrate near zero, the
calibrated threshold stops responding to the risk level and collapses to
uninformative singletons, and pooling the dependent steps of one trajectory
voids the guarantee. We propose a parameter-free normalisation that divides
any base score by one plus the policy's residual confidence and calibrates
over whole episodes, and we prove finite-sample simultaneous coverage over
the entire trajectory at every risk level, with exchangeable episodes as the
single premise; a learning-based weight fits the same theory at the cost of
a calibration split. Across five backbones reproduced to within one point of
their published success rates, the score holds step-averaged coverage above
target in all $45$ runs, its threshold transfers across a $16.4$-point
success-rate spread, and the guarantee carries to REVERIE
navigation. Closing the loop, an operator answering only the steps the
sets flag lifts success from $71\%$ to $91\%$ at a $32\%$ ask rate, a
practical recipe for calibrated help-seeking on embodied agents.
\end{abstract}
